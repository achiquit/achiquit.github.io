Peer Influence and
Social Media for Teens

The basics behind how peer influence and social media interact to create unique effects for teenagers.

Main Text Here


Adolescence can prove to be a difficult time in a person’s life. Adolescents are seen as moody and hormonal, and they are no longer viewed as sweet innocent children. Parents prepare for reckless behavior and conflict as their children start to gain more independence. A defining aspect of adolescence is spending more time with peers which allows for susceptibility of peer influence. As adolescents spend more time with their peers, they grow to be like them in ways that can be both beneficial and harmful. Students can have beneficial influence through academic motivation and improved performance in school. However, there is also a possibility of increased risk behaviors such as substance abuse and health-related concerns.
One relatively new aspect that requires consideration is the effect of social media on peer influence. Social media is defined as any social networking site that allows for interactive, user-generated content typically in the form of image or idea sharing (Chung et al., 2021). Social media can provide global methods of influence and persuasion, especially in the adolescent population. One report found that approximately 85% of adolescents between the ages of 12 and 17 within the United States use a social media website (Hur & Gupta, 2013). Many studies are working to understand the adolescent’s susceptibility to peer influence and specifically the effects of social media on this developing concern.
Multiple studies have researched the ways in which social media use among adolescent peers’ influences eating behaviors. There are examples of positive aspects of peer influence on social media. One study found that young people who are struggling with eating disorders may use online discussion forums as a form of proactive self-care, and they may offer a positive contribution to supporting adolescents with eating disorders (Kendal et al., 2017). However, there are negative cases as well. A separate study found that adolescents use blogs to validate a pro-eating disorder lifestyle, share body and image issues, and search for diets and support from others (Sofia Castro & José Osório, 2013). This is a negative aspect of peer influence on social media. However, these two findings are conflicting, as they both support the idea that adolescents are very susceptible to peer influence which is amplified and made more accessible through social media.
Peer influence can also be observed through apps such as Instagram. One study observed how “Likes” can affect adolescents' neural and behavioral responses to photographs. Adolescents were more likely to ‘Like’ popular photographs and elicited greater brain activation when their own photos received many likes, as compared to few (Sherman et al., 2018). This behavior can be linked to the pattern of adolescent brain maturity. The adolescent brain is still developing, and during this time, it tends to have heightened sensitivity in areas that involve reward processing, specifically the nucleus accumbens (NAcc). The NAcc is linked to behaviors common in social media environments, such as sharing information and receiving positive feedback. There have also been ties to the level of NAcc response to positive social feedback and the intensity of social media use (Sherman et al., 2018). This increase in reward processing is definitely a possible explanation for the increase in peer influence during adolescence because peer acceptance and social rewards will be heightened in the brain.
Due to the nature of adolescence and brain development, it can be inferred that peer influence and reward-seeking would occur with or without the presence of social media. However, social media does add a new aspect that must be considered. It allows for accessible and constant communication, potentially increasing the availability to be influenced by peers. Social media also allows people to interact online with people they would not be able to otherwise. This can be both beneficial and harmful depending on the type of influence being provided. This behavior is likely due to the developmental patterns of the adolescent mind, with the early maturity of the reward processing region. Overall, peer influence appears to be heightened by social media among the adolescent population and should be monitored to prevent possible risky scenarios.
